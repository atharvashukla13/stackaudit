# Reflection — StackAudit

## 1. What was the hardest technical decision you made?

The hardest decision was keeping the audit engine client-side with hardcoded rules instead of using an LLM for recommendations. The temptation to use Claude for generating recommendations was strong — it would have produced more nuanced, natural-language advice and handled edge cases I hadn't anticipated. But I chose hardcoded rules for three reasons. First, every recommendation must trace back to a specific, verifiable pricing comparison — an LLM might hallucinate savings that don't exist, which would destroy user trust immediately. Second, deterministic logic is testable: my 9 Vitest tests verify that the engine produces correct actions for known scenarios, which would be impossible with stochastic LLM output. Third, the assignment explicitly says "knowing when NOT to use AI is part of the test." The trade-off is that my recommendations are less conversational and can't adapt to unusual scenarios, but they're always defensible and auditable — which matters more for a finance tool.

## 2. If you had another week, what would you build next?

I would build three things. First, a **team dashboard** where engineering managers can save their team's stack and get notified when pricing changes affect their recommendations — this transforms a one-time audit into an ongoing relationship. Second, a **historical pricing tracker** that monitors vendor pricing pages weekly and auto-updates the audit engine when plans change, eliminating the manual verification burden. Third, **PDF export** of the audit report, because enterprise users need to share results with finance teams who won't click a URL. The team dashboard would be the highest-impact addition because it turns StackAudit from a tool into a habit, dramatically increasing the surface area for Credex upsells.

## 3. How did you decide what to build vs what to skip?

I applied a simple filter: does this feature directly contribute to someone completing an audit and becoming a Credex lead? The spend input form, audit engine, results page, AI summary, lead capture, and shareable URLs all survived this filter. Things I skipped: user accounts (adds friction, no value for a one-time audit), a comparison matrix page (nice but not core), historical audits (requires auth), and dark/light mode toggle (defaulting to dark is sufficient). The sharpest cut was skipping team management features — multiple users, saved stacks, access controls. These are genuine product features, but they're Day 30 problems, not Day 7 problems. For an MVP whose job is lead generation, the shortest path from "curious visitor" to "qualified lead" is what matters.

## 4. What's one thing you'd do differently?

I would start user interviews on Day 1 instead of Day 3. I treated interviews as a documentation task rather than a product development input, and that was backwards. Early conversations would have shaped the form UX — I later learned that most users think about their AI spend in terms of "total monthly bill" rather than per-tool breakdowns, which means the current form (which asks for per-tool plan selection) might be adding unnecessary friction. An alternative design where users paste their total monthly receipt and we identify the tools could have been more user-friendly. Starting interviews earlier would have also given me time to iterate on the audit engine's recommendation language based on what founders actually find useful versus what sounds technically correct.

## 5. How would you measure success for this product?

Success has two layers. **Product success** is measured by qualified lead volume — specifically, the number of users per week who complete an audit with >$100/mo savings AND submit their email. This is the metric that directly maps to Credex's business goal. Below that, I'd track audit completion rate (are people finishing the form?), email capture rate (is the value proposition compelling enough to trade an email for?), and share rate (are results interesting enough to forward?). **Business success** is measured by Credex consultation bookings originating from StackAudit leads. If the tool generates 10+ warm leads per week at <$1 cost each, and 3-5% convert to Credex customers, the ROI is extraordinary compared to cold outbound. The anti-metric I'd watch is the "optimal" rate — if more than 30% of audits find zero savings, the engine is too conservative and the tool isn't providing enough value to justify the user's time.
